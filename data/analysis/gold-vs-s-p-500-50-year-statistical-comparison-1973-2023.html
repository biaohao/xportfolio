The Diversification Case for Gold

Why gold matters despite lower returns

Correlation of Gold vs S&P 500 (daily, 2000–2026, ETF era): +0.029

Monthly correlation 1973–2023: −0.002

Near-zero correlation means adding gold to a stock portfolio reduces portfolio variance even though gold's standalone Sharpe is lower. Classic mean-variance result: a lower-Sharpe asset with zero correlation always improves a portfolio's efficient frontier.

Summary — What 50 Years Tell Us

S&P 500 wins on total return — by a wide margin
Over 50 years: SP500 delivered 10.45% CAGR vs 6.91% for gold. The compounding gap is enormous — $1 grew to $146 vs $25. SP500 also had lower volatility, higher Sharpe, and was positive 65% of months vs 49% for gold. SP500 wins in 75–83% of rolling 10–20 year windows.
Gold wins in specific regimes — and they are the most painful ones
In the two "lost decades" for stocks (1970s and 2000s), gold delivered +32% and +15% CAGR while stocks were flat or down. These are exactly the periods when a portfolio needs help. Gold's crisis alpha in 1973–74 and 2002 was +40–98 percentage points vs stocks.
Gold's real edge: near-zero correlation with equities
Correlation of −0.002 over 50 years is as close to "uncorrelated" as any real asset gets. This is why a 10–20% gold allocation improves a portfolio's Sharpe ratio even though gold's standalone Sharpe is lower. For a TAA strategy, the SMA-10 timing rule applied to gold further reduces its drawdown from −62% to −33% while improving its own Sharpe from 0.47 to 0.65.
