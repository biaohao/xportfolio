Key Findings & Conclusions

1. Faber's timing rule is confirmed across all three frequencies

The SMA timing rule consistently halves volatility (13% → 6–7%) and reduces max drawdowns by 4× (−40% → −10%). The Sharpe ratio improves from ~0.32 to ~0.55–0.66 regardless of whether monthly, weekly, or daily rebalancing is used.

2. Monthly rebalancing is optimal for this strategy

Monthly (SMA-10) delivers the best Sharpe (0.66) and lowest drawdown (−9.75%) among the three frequencies. Daily and weekly rebalancing introduce more whipsaws — false signals in volatile short-term price movements — that reduce CAGR by ~0.4pp while providing similar risk reduction. This matches the paper's findings.

3. Adding Gold significantly improves the portfolio

Gold's near-zero correlation with equities (+0.03 vs SPY) and CAGR comparable to SPY over 21 years (10.42%) make it an exceptionally valuable addition. The 6-asset portfolio improves B&H max-DD from −46.56% to −39.62% and timing Sharpe from 0.527 to 0.656 (monthly, 2007–2026). The improvement is consistent across all three windows tested.

4. Gold's SMA timing works standalone

Applying the SMA-10 rule to gold alone reduces its max-DD from −62% to −33% while improving CAGR from 8.13% to 8.71% and Sharpe from 0.54 to 0.65. Gold is currently SELL / CASH (−7.7% below its SMA) — consistent across all three frequencies.

5. Commodities (GSG) remain the weakest asset

GSG has delivered −2.73% CAGR over its full history and a −89.62% max-DD. While the timing rule partially protects (moving to cash during the 2008 and 2014–2020 commodity bear markets), the long-run drag from roll costs makes it questionable whether to include it. Consider replacing GSG with a broader commodity ETF (DBC) or dropping it in favour of gold as the commodity/inflation hedge.
