Key Findings

B&H: TLT earns barely more, risks far more
TLT B&H outperforms IEF B&H by just 11bp/yr CAGR (7.44% vs 7.33%), while carrying 22% more volatility and a −26.8% max drawdown vs −23.1%. The risk-adjusted edge goes to IEF: Sharpe 0.421 vs 0.372, Calmar 0.317 vs 0.278.
Timing cuts max drawdown in half
Both timing models roughly halve the max drawdown (IEF: −23% → −13%; TLT: −27% → −12%) and significantly reduce worst-year outcomes (IEF: −16% → −11%; TLT: −25% → −12%). The cost is about 1.6–1.7pp/yr in foregone CAGR — the classic smoothness/return tradeoff.
IEF Timing: most consistent ride
Lowest volatility of all four (5.56%), second-lowest max drawdown (−13.1%), best % positive months (67.0%). Calmar 0.434 — earns the most CAGR per unit of peak loss among the timed portfolios. Ideal for investors who prioritise a smooth equity curve over raw returns.
TLT Timing: best drawdown protection
Tightest max drawdown (−12.3%) and best Calmar ratio (0.478) of all four. The SMA signals exit TLT more aggressively (only 55.7% invested vs 67% for IEF), which limits the 2022 damage to −11.6% vs −24.6% B&H. The tradeoff: misses more of TLT's crisis rallies.
2022 is the acid test. Both B&H portfolios suffered their worst years: IEF B&H −16.3%, TLT B&H −24.6% — the bond component failed as a diversifier in a simultaneous equity+bond selloff. The timing models limited losses to −10.8% and −11.6% respectively, because the SMA signals had already rotated the bond leg into T-bills before the worst rate hikes arrived. This is the core argument for timing over B&H in a rate-sensitive portfolio.
