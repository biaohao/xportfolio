1973–2012 (Faber window) takeaway:
  • Gold's 1980–2001 secular bear dominates: avg drawdown −32%, ratio 1.66 — for every 1% of vol, gold carried 1.66% of average drawdown pain. This is the main reason Faber's timing rule adds so much value on gold.

Cross-Period Summary — Avg DD / Vol Ratio

Higher ratio = more drawdown pain per unit of volatility. A ratio below 0.5 is generally favourable; above 1.0 signals that the asset's drawdowns are disproportionate to its day-to-day swings.

Cross-period key observations:
  • Gold is regime-dependent: ratio flips from ~1.6 (1973–2012, plagued by 19-year bear) to 0.73 (2000–2026, sustained bull). Timing the regime matters enormously — exactly what SMA-10 catches.
  • Long bonds deteriorated sharply: TLT's ratio tripled from 0.36 (1973–2012 bond bull) to 0.89 (2000–2026), making TLT the most painful-per-vol-unit asset in the modern era. IEF followed the same direction but stayed below 0.65 thanks to shorter duration.
  • The S&P 500 is the most stable across periods (0.52–0.66): two large bear markets, two long recoveries, but the ratio stays in a relatively narrow band.
  • The "ideal" asset for a buy-and-hold investor is low avg DD and low ratio — only IEF in 1973–2012 achieves both (−2.1%, 0.26). In 2000–2026 no asset achieves this cleanly; the closest is IEF (−4.4%, 0.65).

Definitions: Avg DD (all months) = arithmetic mean of the monthly drawdown series (0 when at a peak, negative otherwise) — captures both depth and duration of underwater periods. Avg DD (when in DD) = same but restricted to months strictly below prior peak — measures the typical severity of an ongoing drawdown episode. Avg DD / Vol = |average drawdown| divided by annualised volatility — a dimensionless "pain-per-vol" ratio. Values >1 mean the average drawdown exceeds one year of annualised volatility. Ulcer Index = √(mean of squared drawdowns in %) — penalises long deep troughs more than brief dips. All calculations on monthly total-return series, no costs or taxes.
