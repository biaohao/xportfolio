Key Insights from the Full History

#Insight
1 Long-run CAGR settles at ~9.2% — the 152-year figure is the most reliable estimate of the US equity risk premium. The SPY ETF era (1993–2026) shows 10.8% because it captures two of the best decades ever (1990s: 18.4%, 2010s: 13.3%) and is biased high.
2 Two "lost decades" — the 1930s (−0.16%/yr) and 2000s (−0.72%/yr) delivered negative real returns. Both followed periods of extreme valuations (1929 CAPE ~30, 1999 CAPE ~44). This is the empirical foundation for Faber's timing model: a buy-and-hold investor can experience 10-year periods with zero growth.
3 The 1930s had 3× the volatility of a normal decade (30.6% vs ~10–15%) — confirming Faber's observation that volatility clusters and is much higher below the 10-month SMA. The timing model would have sidestepped most of this by exiting in late 1929.
4 The 1950s and 1990s were the two best decades — CAGR of 19.1% and 18.4% with Sharpe ratios of 1.81 and 1.65. Both were characterised by falling interest rates, strong earnings growth, and expanding valuations. Neither is likely to repeat from current valuations.
5 The max drawdown of −81.76% (1929–1932) is the defining risk event — an investor at the 1929 peak would not have recovered to even in nominal terms until 1954 — a 25-year wait. After inflation, real recovery took even longer. This is the ultimate argument for a risk-management overlay like Faber's timing model.
6 Positive years: 73.2% — equities are up roughly 3 years out of 4 over the very long run, but the 27% of down years include some catastrophic losses that devastate compound returns due to the mathematics of loss recovery.
