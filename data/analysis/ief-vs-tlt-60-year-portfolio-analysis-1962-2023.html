Key Insights

1. IEF Timing dominates on risk-adjusted basis

With a Sharpe of 0.88 and Ulcer Performance Index of 2.98, IEF+Timing is the most efficient portfolio over 62 years. Its max drawdown of only −7.6% vs −21.2% buy-and-hold means far less psychological pain — and lower sequence-of-returns risk near retirement.

2. TLT Timing produces the highest final wealth ($322 vs $213)

Long-duration TLT captures larger price swings on rate moves. Timing amplifies this by sidestepping the worst bear markets (1973–74, 1979–81, 2022). But TLT B&H is punishing — the 2022 drawdown reached −31.4% at year-end, second only to 1974's −31.4%.

3. 1982 and 1985 were generational bond bull years

When Volcker-era rates peaked and broke, TLT B&H returned +51% in 1982 and +43% in 1985, with TLT Timing even higher (+57% / +43%). IEF followed with +34% and +34%. This single rate cycle accounts for a disproportionate share of the 62-year compounding advantage.

4. Timing adds the most value in rising-rate environments

In 1969 (−9.3% IEF B&H → +1.4% IEF Timing), 1974 (−12.1% → +7.3%), 1981 (−1.2% → +12.2%) and 2022 (−14.8% → −2.0%), the SMA-10 filter cut losses by 8–16 percentage points. The cost: lagging in sharp recovery years like 1975 (+20.8% vs +6.7%).

5. IEF vs TLT: stability vs optionality

IEF's shorter duration (~7yr) keeps it in a narrower return band: 62-year range roughly −15% to +34%. TLT's ~16yr duration widens that to −23% to +57%. For a taxable buy-and-hold investor without timing, IEF's lower volatility (7.6% vs 10.4%), shallower drawdowns, and near-identical long-run CAGR (8.2% vs 8.1%) make it the more practical choice. Timing changes that calculus in TLT's favour.

6. 2022 stress test

The fastest rate-hiking cycle in 40 years hit TLT B&H for −23.2% (portfolio) — worse than 2008's −8.2%. IEF B&H lost −14.8%. Both timing strategies contained damage: IEF Timing −2.0%, TLT Timing −2.9%. The SMA-10 signal triggered exit in January 2022 and kept both portfolios largely in T-bills through the carnage.
