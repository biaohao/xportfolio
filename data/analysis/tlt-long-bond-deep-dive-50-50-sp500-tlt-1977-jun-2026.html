The bond bull is dead — for now. TLT earned positive CAGR in every decade from the 1980s through the 2010s as rates fell from 15% to 0%. The 1977–79 and 2020–26 decades both show negative CAGR driven by rising rates. This is the key risk: long bonds are not a standalone investment — they are a hedge that works only in deflationary/recessionary environments.

Critical insight: The negative SP500 correlation that makes TLT valuable as a diversifier only existed from 2000–2019. In the 1980s–90s and again in the 2020s the correlation was positive — meaning bonds and stocks both fell together (2022: SP500 −18%, TLT −31%). The SMA timing rule partially protects against this: it signalled out of TLT in early 2022 before the worst losses.

But 2022 shows the opposite: when inflation is the crisis driver (not deflation/recession), long bonds are the problem, not the solution. The SMA-10 timing rule was critical in 2022 — it moved TLT to cash early enough to limit the timed strategy's loss to −2.6% vs −31.2% buy-and-hold.

Key Conclusions

1
TLT is not a standalone investment — it's a portfolio tool. A 5.77% CAGR over 49 years with −67% max-DD and Sharpe 0.18 is a poor standalone result. Its value is entirely in its negative correlation with equities (−0.30 mean), which only materialised reliably from 2000–2019. In the 1980s–90s and 2020s the correlation was positive.
2
SMA timing is essential for TLT — more so than for equities or gold. Timing cuts TLT's max-DD from −68% to −24% and improves CAGR from 5.77% to 8.05% over the full period. In 2022, timing limited losses to −2.6% vs −31.2% B&H. Without timing, a buy-and-hold TLT position is extremely painful to hold through rate-rise cycles.
3
50/50 SP500+TLT Timing is the smoothest ride but not the highest return. Max-DD of only −12.34% over 49 years (including 2022!), Ulcer Index 3.57%, and worst year −11.6%. But CAGR is 10.16% vs 11.58% for SP500-only Timing — a 1.4 pp annual cost for the smoother ride.
4
The negative correlation is not guaranteed going forward. In the 2020s it flipped back to positive as inflation drove both bonds and stocks lower. In a stagflationary environment, TLT provides no crisis hedge — unlike gold, which retained its diversification value. This is the fundamental difference between TLT and GLD as portfolio diversifiers.
5
Gold vs TLT as the diversifier: Gold dominates TLT as a diversifier in the 2007–2026 period: Gold B&H CAGR 9.47% vs TLT 3.12%; Gold+SP500 50/50 Timing Sharpe 1.064 vs TLT+SP500 0.641. For the long-run 1977–2026 period they are closer. A 3-asset portfolio (SP500 + Gold + TLT) combining both diversifiers is the natural next step.
