7 · Interpretation

Full history (2010–2026): SMA-6 wins on CAGR (+125%) mainly because it stays invested longer during the ultra-thin hyper-growth 2010–2013 Mt.Gox era — any momentum signal was nearly irrelevant when Bitcoin was rising 5,000%+ in a single year. CAGR declines monotonically from SMA-6 to SMA-12 then partially recovers. Volatility drops steadily as lookback lengthens (165% → 136%), reflecting fewer round-trips. All SMA periods beat B&H on max drawdown (B&H −82% vs timing −65% to −70%).

ETF Era (2014–2026): SMA-11 is the standout — highest CAGR (+69.6%), best Sharpe (1.137), and max DD of just −50.7% vs B&H's −76%. SMA-10 is nearly identical (CAGR +67.2%, Sharpe 1.118). The SMA-6 to SMA-9 cluster is more reactive — faster to re-enter after crashes — but slower to exit in the early drop phase, resulting in deeper drawdowns (−50% to −65%). SMA-12 to SMA-15 adds lag that misses recoveries, hurting CAGR without providing meaningfully better DD protection. The 10–11 month window sits at the signal-to-noise sweet spot for Bitcoin's ~4-year halving cycle periodicity.

Volatility is nearly invariant across all lookbacks (59–62% in the ETF era), confirming the SMA filter only controls when you are invested, not the character of the per-period returns while invested. The primary lever is drawdown avoidance.

% Time invested grows monotonically from 58% (SMA-6) to 68% (SMA-15). Longer SMAs spend more time invested but the incremental return from the extra invested periods is not enough to compensate for the lag in exiting bear markets, explaining the CAGR peak at SMA-10/11 rather than at SMA-15.

Robustness caveat: these are in-sample results over a 10-year window containing exactly two major bear markets (2018, 2022). The broad conclusion — any SMA from 8 to 13 months materially improves risk-adjusted returns over B&H — is robust. The precise ranking within that range is not statistically reliable and should not be used to select a specific parameter without out-of-sample validation.
