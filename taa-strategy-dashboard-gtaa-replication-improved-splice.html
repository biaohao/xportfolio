Key Findings & Conclusions

1. Faber's timing rule is confirmed across all three frequencies

The SMA timing rule consistently cuts volatility in half (11–14% → 6–7%) and reduces max drawdowns by 4× (−40% → −10%). The Sharpe ratio improves from ~0.36–0.46 to ~0.54–0.60 regardless of whether monthly, weekly, or daily rebalancing is used. The core result is robust to the improved bond splice.

2. Monthly rebalancing is optimal for this strategy

Monthly (SMA-10) delivers the best Sharpe (0.597) and lowest drawdown (−9.75%) among the three frequencies. Daily and weekly introduce more whipsaws that reduce CAGR by ~0.1pp while providing similar risk reduction. This matches Faber's findings exactly.

3. Adding Gold consistently improves the portfolio

Gold's near-zero correlation with equities (+0.064 vs SPY) and CAGR comparable to SPY since 2004 (10.27%) make it an exceptionally valuable addition. The 6-asset timing Sharpe improves from 0.532 → 0.597 (+0.065) in the 2007–2026 window. The improvement is consistent across all three windows and all frequencies.

4. Splice quality matters for pre-ETF history

Correcting the bond duration from D=8.0 to D=7.35 (empirically optimal, −30% RMSE reduction) produces a more faithful pre-2002 bond return series. The change has a modest effect on the 2007–2026 backtest (IEF is ETF-only in that window) but improves signal accuracy in the 1962–2002 long-history period used for the full-paper 1973–2012 replication.

5. Commodities (GSG) remain the weakest asset

GSG has delivered −2.85% CAGR over its full ETF history and a −88.68% max-DD. While the timing rule partially protects, the structural drag from futures roll costs makes it questionable whether to include it long-term. Gold already serves as the inflation/commodity hedge in the 6-asset universe.
